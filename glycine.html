GLYCINE

What is Glycine?

Glycine is an inhibitory neurotransmitter primarily found in the spinal cord and brainstem. It plays a crucial role in regulating motor control and preventing excessive neuronal excitation.

In the brain, glycine also acts as a co-agonist for NMDA receptors, meaning it helps glutamate activate certain excitatory pathways

Main Functions

  • Inhibits motor neurons

    Glycine helps control muscle movement by preventing overactivation of motor neurons, ensuring smooth and coordinated motion.

  • Balances excitation

    It counteracts excitatory neurotransmitters like glutamate, maintaining neural stability.

  • Supports reflex regulation

    Glycine is essential in spinal reflex circuits, helping regulate automatic movements.

  • Modulates NMDA receptors

    In higher brain regions, glycine assists glutamate at NMDA receptors, which are important for learning and memory.

If Levels Are Too Low

  • Muscle stiffness or spasms

    • Insufficient inhibition can lead to excessive motor activity.

  • Incereased neural excitability

    • The nervous system may become overly reactive

If Levels Are Too High

  • Reduce neural activity

    • Excess inhibition can suppres normal neural signaling.

  • Sedation-like effects

    • High inhibitory activity may contribute to decreased alertness.

Interesting Facts

🧬Glycine receptors are especially abundant in the spinal cord.

🧬The toxin strychnine blocks glycine receptors, leading to severe muscle spasms.

🧬Glycine is also an amino acid used in protein sythesis.

Endorphins

Neuropeptides involved in pain relief and feelings of well-being.

Overview

Endorphins are endogenous opioid neuropeptides produced by the central nervous system. They function primarily in pain modulation and the regulation of emotional states, often contributing to sensations of pleasure and well-being.

Synthesis

Endorphins are derived from precursor proteins such as proopiomelanocortin (POMC). They are released in response to stress, pain, exercise, and other physiological stimuli.

Receptors

Endorphins bind primarily to opioid receptors (mu, delta, and kappa receptors), which are G-protein-coupled receptors that inhibit neuronal activity and reduce pain signaling.

Functions

  • Pain suppression (analgesic effect)
  • Stress reduction
  • Emotional regulation
  • Exercise-induced euphoria ("runner’s high")

Clinical Relevance

  • Chronic pain conditions
  • Depression
  • Addiction mechanisms
  • Stress-related disorders

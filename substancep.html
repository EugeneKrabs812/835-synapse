Substance P

A neuropeptide involved in pain transmission and inflammatory signaling.

Overview

Substance P is a neuropeptide that functions as both a neurotransmitter and neuromodulator. It plays a central role in the transmission of pain signals from peripheral nerves to the central nervous system and is also involved in inflammatory responses.

Synthesis

Substance P is produced from the TAC1 gene and belongs to the tachykinin peptide family. It is synthesized in neuronal cell bodies and transported to axon terminals, where it is released in response to noxious stimuli.

Receptors

Substance P primarily binds to the neurokinin-1 (NK1) receptor, a G-protein-coupled receptor. Activation of NK1 receptors enhances pain perception and promotes inflammatory signaling pathways.

Functions

  • Transmission of pain signals (nociception)
  • Regulation of inflammatory responses
  • Modulation of stress and anxiety responses
  • Influence on nausea and vomiting pathways

Clinical Relevance

  • Chronic pain disorders
  • Inflammatory diseases
  • Depression and stress-related conditions
  • Target of NK1 receptor antagonist drugs (antiemetics)

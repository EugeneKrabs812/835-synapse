ASPARTATE

A lesser-known excitatory neurotransmitter that enhances neural signaling and contributes to synaptic plasticity.

What is Aspartate

Aspartate is an excitatory neurotransmitter found in the central nervous system. It plays a role in stimulating neurons and supporting neural communication.

Although less prominent than glutamate, aspartate contributes to excitatory signaling and is involved in learning and memory processes.

Main Functions

  • Promotes excitatory signaling

    Aspartate stimulates neurons by activating excitatory receptors, increasing the likelihood that a neuron will fire.

  • Supports learning and memory

    It interacts with NMDA receptors, which are important for synaptic plasticity —the brain's ability to strengthen connections over time.

  • Contributes to neural development

    Aspartate is involved in early brain development and neural network formation.

  • Participates in metabolic processes

    Aspartate is also an amino acid that plays a role in cellular energy cycles, linking neurotransmitter to metabolism.

If Levels Are Too Low

  • Reduced excitatory activitity — Neural signaling may become less responsive
  • Slower cognitive processing — Decreased stimulation could impact information transmission efficiency

If Levels Are Too High

  • Excessive neural excitation

    Overactivation may contribute to excitotoxicity — a condition where neurons are demaged due to overstrimulation.

  • Increased seizure risk

    Excess excitatory activity can destabilize neural networks.

Interesting facts

🧬Aspartate is structurally similar to glutamate.

🧬It acts on NMDA-type receptors.

🧬Like glycine and glutamate, it is also an amino acid used in protein synthesis.

Epinephrine

A catecholamine hormone and neurotransmitter central to the fight-or-flight response.

Overview

Epinephrine, also known as adrenaline, is a catecholamine produced primarily by the adrenal medulla. It functions both as a hormone in the bloodstream and as a neurotransmitter in the central nervous system, rapidly preparing the body for acute stress or emergency situations.

Synthesis

Epinephrine is synthesized from the amino acid tyrosine through a multistep pathway: tyrosine → L-DOPA → dopamine → norepinephrine → epinephrine. The final step is catalyzed by the enzyme phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase (PNMT), primarily in the adrenal medulla.

Receptors

Epinephrine binds to adrenergic receptors (alpha and beta subtypes), which are G-protein-coupled receptors. Activation of these receptors leads to increased heart rate, bronchodilation, vasoconstriction or vasodilation depending on receptor subtype, and mobilization of glucose.

Functions

  • Increases heart rate and cardiac output
  • Dilates airways (bronchodilation)
  • Mobilizes glucose for rapid energy
  • Enhances alertness and reaction speed
  • Redirects blood flow to skeletal muscles

Clinical Relevance

  • Anaphylaxis treatment (epinephrine injection)
  • Cardiac arrest management
  • Asthma and bronchospasm treatment
  • Stress and anxiety physiology
